## How they overlap

ADHD and autism are both neurodevelopmental, meaning they show up early in development and shape how the brain processes the world. They share real common ground. Both can involve differences in [executive function](/terms/executive-function/), so planning, switching tasks, and managing time can be hard in either. Both can come with [sensory overload](/terms/sensory-overload/), social friction, and a tendency to [mask](/terms/masking/) struggles to fit in. Many people meet criteria for both at once, and the same person can carry traits of each. Emotional sensitivity, including [rejection sensitive dysphoria](/terms/rejection-sensitive-dysphoria/), is common across both as well, which adds to the blur.

## How they actually differ

The core of each condition sits in a different place. ADHD centers on attention regulation, impulse control, and activity level. The classic picture is a mind that drifts, jumps between tasks, and seeks stimulation, paired with restlessness or impulsive choices. Autism centers on social communication and a pattern of restricted, repetitive behavior. The classic picture there is genuine difficulty reading social cues, plus a strong pull toward routine, sameness, and deep focused interests.

Attention itself can look opposite. An autistic person may lock intensely onto a topic for hours, while ADHD attention tends to scatter. Routine also splits them. ADHD often craves novelty, while autism often leans on predictability for comfort.

## When it's one and when it's the other

If the main story is distractibility, restlessness, and acting before thinking, that pattern points toward ADHD. If the main story is social communication differences, a strong need for routine, and intense narrow interests, that points toward autism. Because the two overlap and frequently coexist, the honest answer is often both, and a careful developmental history matters more than any single behavior.

## Why the distinction matters

Getting the picture right shapes support. ADHD care often involves strategies for attention and impulse control, sometimes including medication. Autism support often focuses on communication, sensory needs, and accommodations that respect a person's need for predictability. When both are present, ignoring either one leaves real needs unmet. A thorough evaluation, rather than a quick label, gives a person the support that actually fits how their brain works.
