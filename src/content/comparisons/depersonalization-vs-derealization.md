## How they overlap

Depersonalization and derealization are two sides of the same coin, and they often arrive together. Both are forms of [dissociation](/terms/dissociation/), a sense of disconnection that the mind can produce when it's overwhelmed. Both tend to be set off by the same things: intense [anxiety](/terms/anxiety/), a panic attack, acute stress, exhaustion, or trauma. And in both, reality testing stays intact, meaning the person knows the strange feeling isn't literally true even while it's happening. That preserved awareness is what makes the experience so unsettling and is part of why people often describe both with the same word, unreal. Because they overlap so heavily, the diagnostic manual groups them under a single condition rather than two.

## How they actually differ

The dividing line is simple once you see it: one is about the self, the other is about the world.

Depersonalization is the sense of being detached from yourself. People describe watching their own life from the outside, feeling like a spectator to their thoughts and actions, or feeling that their body, voice, or emotions don't quite belong to them. Hands might look unfamiliar; feelings might seem muffled or robotic.

Derealization turns that same detachment outward. Here the surroundings feel unreal. The world can look foggy, flat, dreamlike, or as if seen through glass. Familiar places feel strange, colors and sounds seem off, and other people can appear distant or lifeless. The self feels intact, but everything outside it seems somehow not quite there.

## When it's one and when it's the other

If the strangeness is centered on you, your body, your thoughts, your sense of being a real person, that's depersonalization. If the strangeness is centered on your environment, the room, the street, the people in it, that's derealization. Many people feel both at once, which is normal and expected. Brief episodes during high [anxiety](/terms/anxiety/) or after a frightening event are common and not in themselves a disorder. When the feelings become persistent or recurrent and cause real distress, they may point toward [depersonalization-derealization disorder](/terms/depersonalization-derealization-disorder/).

## Why the distinction matters

Putting accurate words to the experience helps in two ways. First, it's reassuring: knowing that these are recognized, named forms of dissociation, and that reality testing staying intact is part of the picture, can take some of the fear out of them. Second, it helps a clinician find the cause. These feelings often ride alongside anxiety, panic, or trauma, and sometimes alongside [hypervigilance](/terms/hypervigilance/), so describing whether the unreality is about you or about the world helps point toward what's driving it and what kind of support fits best.
