## How they overlap

ADD and ADHD describe the same underlying condition, so the overlap is nearly complete. Both point to lifelong differences in attention and self-regulation that begin in childhood. Both involve [executive function](/terms/executive-function/) challenges, where holding plans in mind, resisting distraction, and following through take real effort. The honest summary is that ADD isn't a separate thing from ADHD at all. It's an older word for part of the same picture, which is why people use the two interchangeably even though only one is current.

## How they actually differ

The difference is history and language, not biology. ADD, short for attention deficit disorder, was the official term decades ago for people whose main struggle was inattention without obvious hyperactivity. When the diagnostic system was updated, that label was retired. Everything was folded under the single name ADHD, which now includes three presentations: predominantly inattentive, predominantly hyperactive or impulsive, and combined.

So what someone once would have called ADD is today described as ADHD, inattentive presentation. The person who quietly loses track of tasks, struggles with [working memory](/terms/working-memory/), and drifts off mid-conversation fits that profile. Hyperactivity and weak [inhibition](/terms/inhibition/) sit more on the hyperactive side of the umbrella, but all of it is ADHD now.

## When it's one and when it's the other

Since ADD is no longer an official diagnosis, the practical answer is that it's always ADHD today. If attention and follow-through are the main problem and there's little outward restlessness, that's the inattentive presentation of ADHD, the pattern people used to call ADD. If impulsivity and hyperactivity are prominent, that's the hyperactive or combined presentation. The condition is the same either way, just sorted by which features stand out.

## Why the distinction matters

Using the current name matters for getting accurate information and care. Searching for ADD can lead to dated material, while ADHD points to up to date guidance and treatment. It also helps people understand that quiet, inattentive struggles count as fully as the restless, impulsive kind. Someone who never seemed hyperactive can still have ADHD, and naming it correctly opens the door to support that fits.
