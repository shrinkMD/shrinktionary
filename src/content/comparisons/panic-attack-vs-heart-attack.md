## How they overlap

This is the rare comparison where the overlap is the most important part. A [panic attack](/terms/panic-attack/) and a heart attack can produce strikingly similar symptoms: chest pain or pressure, a pounding or racing heart, shortness of breath, sweating, nausea, dizziness, and a powerful sense that something is terribly wrong. During a panic attack, the body's [fight-or-flight](/terms/fight-or-flight/) response floods the system, and rapid breathing or [hyperventilation](/terms/hyperventilation/) can add to the chest tightness. Because the sensations are so close, many people who are actually having a panic attack fear they're dying, and many people having a heart attack hope it's "just anxiety." You cannot reliably tell the two apart on your own. If there's any chest pain or any doubt, treat it as a medical emergency and call 911 or go to emergency care.

## How they actually differ

Underneath the similar feelings, the two are very different events. A panic attack is a surge of intense fear driven by the nervous system. It's deeply unpleasant but not physically dangerous, and it typically peaks within about ten minutes and then begins to ease. A heart attack is caused by blocked blood flow to the heart muscle. It's a life-threatening emergency, and its symptoms often persist or worsen rather than passing on their own. Some patterns make a heart attack more likely, such as pain that spreads to the arm, jaw, or back, pain brought on by exertion, or symptoms in someone with cardiac risk factors. But these are tendencies, not guarantees, and no checklist replaces medical evaluation.

## When it's one and when it's the other

A clinician can distinguish them with tests like an ECG and blood work; a person in the moment usually can't. If you have a known history of panic attacks, the episode matches your past attacks exactly, and it eases within minutes, it may well be panic. Even then, new, different, or severe chest pain deserves emergency assessment. The safe default is simple: when in doubt, assume it could be your heart. Calling 911 for a panic attack is far better than dismissing a heart attack as anxiety.

## Why the distinction matters

Getting this right is a matter of safety, not labeling. A heart attack needs treatment within minutes, so hesitation costs more than embarrassment. At the same time, repeated panic attacks are treatable, and chronic fear that an attack is a heart attack can point toward [panic disorder](/terms/panic-disorder/) or [illness anxiety disorder](/terms/illness-anxiety-disorder/), which have effective care. The goal is to take every possible cardiac event seriously while also getting the right help for anxiety once the heart has been cleared.
