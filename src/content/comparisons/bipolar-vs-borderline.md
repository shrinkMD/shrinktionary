## How they overlap

Bipolar disorder and borderline personality disorder both feature powerful emotions and trouble keeping mood steady, so on the surface they can look alike. Both can involve impulsive choices, irritability, and stretches of deep low mood. Both can include thoughts of self harm, and both can disrupt work and relationships. [Emotional dysregulation](/terms/emotional-dysregulation/), the difficulty of managing strong feelings, runs through each one. Because the two share so many surface features, they're among the more commonly confused diagnoses, and a person can even have both at the same time.

## How they actually differ

The clearest divide is timing. Bipolar disorder runs in episodes. A period of [mania](/terms/mania/) or [hypomania](/terms/hypomania/), marked by elevated energy, reduced need for sleep, and racing activity, lasts days to weeks, as do the depressive periods. Between episodes, mood often returns to a steady baseline. The shifts frequently arrive on their own, not in response to a specific event.

Borderline personality disorder moves much faster. Mood can swing within hours, and the swings are usually tied to relationships and the fear of being abandoned or rejected. Rather than week long highs, there's intense, shifting reactivity, often alongside an unstable sense of self. Bipolar highs include real mania, which borderline does not produce.

## When it's one and when it's the other

If mood states last for days or weeks, include clear manic or hypomanic energy, and shift without an obvious trigger, that pattern points toward bipolar disorder. If mood flips within a single day, tracks closely with relationship stress, and comes with a shaky self image and fear of abandonment, that points toward borderline personality disorder. The pace of change and the role of triggers are the most useful clues, and a careful history matters more than any single bad day.

## Why the distinction matters

The treatments differ in emphasis. Bipolar disorder usually responds best to a [mood stabilizer](/terms/mood-stabilizer/) and ongoing medical management. Borderline personality disorder responds best to structured psychotherapy, with [dialectical behavior therapy](/terms/dbt/) as a leading approach. Mislabeling one as the other can send someone toward treatment that doesn't fit. Getting the diagnosis right, including recognizing when both are present, points a person toward the care most likely to help.
