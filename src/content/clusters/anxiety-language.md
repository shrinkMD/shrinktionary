Anxiety usually starts in the body before it has a name. The [fight-or-flight](/terms/fight-or-flight/) response switches on, breathing speeds up into [hyperventilation](/terms/hyperventilation/), and the mind starts [catastrophizing](/terms/catastrophizing/), running every situation to its worst outcome. When that alarm fires suddenly and peaks within minutes, it's a [panic attack](/terms/panic-attack/). When it stays switched on, scanning for threats that aren't there, it's [hypervigilance](/terms/hypervigilance/).

The conditions in this cluster are the named patterns those experiences fall into, from [generalized anxiety disorder](/terms/generalized-anxiety-disorder/) to [social anxiety disorder](/terms/social-anxiety-disorder/) and the specific fears of a [phobia](/terms/specific-phobia/). The thread that connects most of them is [avoidance](/terms/avoidance/), which feels like relief but quietly makes anxiety stronger.

The treatments share a vocabulary too. [Exposure therapy](/terms/exposure-therapy/) and [CBT](/terms/cbt/) are the most studied approaches, and [SSRIs](/terms/ssri/) are a common first-line medication. Knowing these words is the first step to understanding what's happening and what helps.
