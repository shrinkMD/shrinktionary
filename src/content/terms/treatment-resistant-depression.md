## What treatment-resistant depression actually is

Treatment-resistant depression is the term for depression that hasn't responded well enough after a fair trial of treatment, usually defined as at least two different antidepressants taken at an adequate dose for an adequate length of time. The label signals that the usual first steps haven't done the job.

It's a common and recognized situation. Putting a name to it isn't a verdict. It's a cue to step back and rethink the approach.

## How it works in practice

When depression isn't responding, a clinician first checks the basics. Was the dose high enough? Was it taken long enough? Were doses missed? Is something else going on, like an undiagnosed condition or another illness adding to the picture? Sometimes what looks like resistance is really an incomplete trial.

Once those are sorted out, the plan shifts. Options can include switching medications, combining approaches, adding therapy, or considering other treatments. The point of the label is to trigger this fresh look rather than repeating the same thing and hoping for a different result.

## What it isn't

It isn't a sign that someone is broken or beyond help. Many people with treatment-resistant depression improve once the plan changes.

It also isn't a diagnosis you give yourself after one medication didn't work. The term has a specific meaning tied to adequate trials, and sorting that out is a clinician's job.

## Why it matters

The label matters because it changes the next step. Naming depression as treatment-resistant pushes a clinician to look harder at what's been tried, rule out hidden factors, and move toward a different strategy. It reframes a frustrating stretch as a turning point rather than a dead end, and it opens the door to options that go beyond the first round of treatment.
