## What akathisia actually is

Akathisia is a feeling of intense inner restlessness, usually paired with a hard-to-resist urge to move. People describe it as not being able to sit still, a buzzing under the skin, or a sense that they have to keep shifting, pacing, or rocking to get relief.

It's a known side effect of some psychiatric medications, especially certain antipsychotics, though it can show up with other drugs too. The discomfort is real and can be quite distressing.

## What it looks like

From the outside, akathisia can look like fidgeting, pacing, foot-tapping, rocking, or an inability to stay seated. From the inside, it feels worse than it looks. The driving sensation is an uncomfortable, sometimes overwhelming need to move.

It often starts soon after a medication is begun or a dose is raised. Because the inner distress can be mistaken for anxiety or worsening illness, naming it correctly matters. The treatment for restlessness caused by a medication is different from treating an anxiety flare.

## What it isn't

Akathisia isn't ordinary anxiety, even though it can feel similar. The key clue is the physical, motor-driven urge to move and its timing right after a medication change.

It also isn't the same as tardive dyskinesia, which involves involuntary movements that tend to develop later. The two are different problems, even though both relate to movement and medication.

## What to do about it

Akathisia can be serious and very distressing, and it deserves prompt contact with a prescriber. Don't just push through it or stop the medication on your own. A clinician can confirm what's happening and adjust the plan, since lowering a dose or changing the approach often helps. Reporting it early, especially soon after starting or increasing a medication, gives the best chance of quick relief.
